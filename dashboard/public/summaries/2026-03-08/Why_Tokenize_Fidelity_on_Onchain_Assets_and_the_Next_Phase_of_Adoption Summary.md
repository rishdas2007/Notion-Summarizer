## Executive Summary

This episode of the a16z crypto show features Cynthia Lo Bessette, Head of Digital Asset Management at Fidelity Investments, discussing Fidelity's strategic approach to digital asset adoption and tokenization. The conversation centers on a three-phase adoption framework—Hold, Use, and Build—that guides Fidelity's product roadmap from Bitcoin ETPs to tokenized money market funds and beyond.

**Core Argument:** Tokenization represents more than technical migration of assets to blockchain infrastructure. The critical challenge lies in creating genuine onchain utility that differentiates tokenized assets from their traditional counterparts. Fidelity's approach prioritizes answering fundamental questions: why bring an asset onchain, what can it do onchain that it cannot do off-chain, and who will use it.

**Key Strategic Elements:**

- **Phase 1 (Hold):** Providing traditional investors access to digital assets through familiar wrappers like ETPs
- **Phase 2 (Use):** Enabling onchain functionality including collateralization, capital access, enhanced tradability, and native yield generation through staking
- **Phase 3 (Build):** Constructing hyper-personalized portfolios using both tokenized traditional assets and native onchain assets

The discussion reveals that Fidelity has invested more time building ecosystem relationships and utility infrastructure than in the technical token issuance itself. Market crises—specifically the regional banking crisis and USDC stablecoin depegging—accelerated adoption of tokenized treasury products by highlighting systemic vulnerabilities in traditional payment and yield systems.

## Detailed Analysis

### The Three-Phase Framework for Digital Asset Adoption

Fidelity structures its digital asset strategy through a sequential adoption model that reflects increasing levels of onchain integration and sophistication.

**Phase 1: Hold**

The initial phase focuses on accessibility and exposure. Bessette explains: "How do we help our clients to be able to get exposure to this asset class? And that would be under the hold phase of adoption. So how do we help investors to get access to trade these assets and hold them in their portfolios?"

Exchange-traded products (ETPs) serve as the primary vehicle for this phase, functioning as "a fantastic way to introduce traditional investors to these on-chain assets." This approach leverages existing distribution infrastructure while providing exposure to onchain assets through familiar investment wrappers. The strategy recognizes that traditional investors require entry points that align with their current operational frameworks and regulatory comfort zones.

**Phase 2: Use**

The second phase represents a fundamental shift from passive holding to active utility. Bessette emphasizes: "Where tokenizing assets is really not the hardest part of bringing assets on-chain, it's making these tokenized assets useful to investors, and useful to investors in ways that are different from the way in which they can think of these assets in their brokerage accounts today."

This phase encompasses several distinct use cases:

1. **Mobility and Collateralization:** Making assets "mobile on-chain, allowing investors to use them to deposit as collateral, to be able to get access to capital on-chain"
2. **Enhanced Tradability:** Enabling assets to be "more easily tradable into a more highly portfolio" (note: the transcript appears incomplete here, likely referring to diversified or liquid portfolios)
3. **Native Yield Generation:** Providing "the ability to generate yield on-chain" through staking, which Bessette describes as "incredibly differentiated from traditional sources of yield"

The packaging of onchain staking yield into traditional ETP wrappers exemplifies Fidelity's bridge-building approach—delivering novel onchain functionality through familiar investment structures.

**Phase 3: Build**

The final phase envisions comprehensive onchain portfolio construction. Bessette describes this as having "on-chain assets in an on-chain wrapper and the ability to be able to deliver hyper-personalized exposures to investors with these both tokenized traditional assets as well as native on-chain assets and then being able to construct portfolios on-chain."

This phase represents full integration where both the assets and the investment vehicles exist natively onchain, enabling customization and portfolio management capabilities that transcend traditional limitations.

### Tokenization Strategy: Utility Before Technology

Bessette identifies the fundamental question that must precede any tokenization initiative: "Why are you bringing that asset on-chain? What does it, you know to do on-chain that it can't do today in its current form? And who are you doing it for? And how is that person going to be able to use that asset on-chain?"

This utility-first framework challenges the assumption that tokenization inherently creates value. The progression Fidelity observes in market evolution moves from "on-chain assets in an off-chain wrapper, which is the ETP" using "existing infrastructure for distribution to be able to deliver on-chain exposures" toward native onchain distribution infrastructure.

However, Bessette notes that "the on-chain infrastructure for distribution is really at the very beginning right now," indicating significant development remains necessary before the full vision can be realized.

### Ecosystem Development and Infrastructure Challenges

Fidelity's experience launching its tokenized money market fund reveals that technical token issuance represents only a fraction of the total effort required. Bessette states: "We've spent, you know, a good portion of time building out the infrastructure to be able to support the issuance of the token. I think we've probably spent more time building out both the relationships as well as some of the ecosystem development that we need to build in order to make this token more usable on chain."

This observation highlights a critical insight: the majority of work in bringing tokenized products to market involves ecosystem coordination, relationship building, and creating the conditions for utility rather than the technical blockchain implementation itself. The infrastructure challenge extends beyond internal systems to encompass:

- Partnership development with ecosystem participants
- Integration with existing onchain protocols and platforms
- Creating pathways for actual usage rather than mere existence of tokens
- Building distribution channels that can deliver onchain assets effectively

### Crisis-Driven Market Evolution

Two specific crisis events catalyzed rapid growth in tokenized cash products and fundamentally shifted market understanding of tokenization's value proposition.

**Regional Banking Crisis and USDC Depegging**

Bessette explains: "Post the small and regional bank mini crisis that happened a couple of years ago with the de-pegging of a very large, well-known stable coin. USDC. Yes. I think there was a recognition from a market standpoint that the banking system needed to be looked at from the standpoint of not just access, which is, I think, where a lot of the early thesis was focused, but also on the flow of capital and the payment system."

These events created an "aha moment for the use of tokenized money market funds as a way to be able to bridge that payments and yield gap." The crisis revealed a structural inefficiency: traditional brokerage platforms sweep cash into interest-bearing products, but many payment platforms held cash without generating yield. This disconnect "doesn't really resonate with most investors and users."

**Regulatory Catalyst: The Genius Act**

Following the initial crisis recognition, "the Genius Act really just absolutely opened up the marketplace for non-bank issuers to really think about how do we facilitate payments more easily on our platforms." Tokenized money market funds emerged as critical infrastructure, "play[ing] a very important role for the reserve assets and being able to stay on chain for stablecoin issuers and their underlying users."

This regulatory development transformed tokenized money market funds from theoretical products to essential infrastructure for the emerging onchain payment ecosystem.

### Institutional Decision-Making: Build vs. Partner

Fidelity's approach to technology development reflects a strategic framework for determining which capabilities to develop internally versus sourcing through partnerships.

**The Kaizen Philosophy and Bitcoin Mining**

Bessette references Fidelity's early Bitcoin mining experiments as exemplifying the company's "Kaizen" philosophy of "constant and continuous improvement in yourself and in your business." She explains: "This idea of, you know, mining Bitcoin wasn't just suddenly somebody waking up one morning and say, hey, I should, you know, mine Bitcoin. But rather, it started with this curiosity and saying, I just heard about this new thing called Bitcoin. I'm not quite sure whether it's a thing, whether it's not a thing, but I should make it my job to figure out whether or not it is something."

The mining experiment served as a learning mechanism: "You have to deconstruct something, I think, oftentimes in order to figure out how to put it back together again, to really truly understand how it works." This hands-on approach to understanding new technologies informs Fidelity's subsequent build-versus-partner decisions.

**Strategic Framework for Build Decisions**

Bessette articulates the decision framework: "You've got to evaluate your operating platform in the context of what it is that you're looking to build and what are the most important points of differentiation for your product to your client? What are the touch points that you will have with your client?"

The guiding principle: "Those elements of the operating platform that give us the most control to be able to pivot and to be flexible and being able to build new functions to really differentiate our product offering, those would be the parts of our operating platform that we would tend to want to build."

This framework prioritizes internal development for capabilities that:
- Provide competitive differentiation
- Require flexibility and rapid iteration
- Represent critical client touchpoints
- Enable strategic pivoting

Conversely, commoditized or non-differentiating capabilities become candidates for partnership or vendor relationships.

### Founder Evaluation and Partnership Criteria

When evaluating potential partnerships with crypto founders and projects, Fidelity employs a multi-dimensional assessment framework.

**Vision and Ecosystem Fit**

The evaluation "starts with the premise of not only what are you building or what is the project about, but what's the vision for where that project fits in the bigger picture ecosystem?" This holistic perspective ensures partnerships align with Fidelity's broader strategic objectives for bringing customers "into the ecosystem to get exposure, both to assets on chain, as well as to be able to get access to capital on chain."

**Regulatory Understanding**

Founders must demonstrate "a good understanding of the regulatory environment that we are looking to operate within" while also helping Fidelity "think about those principles as we apply them in this new market environment." This dual requirement—respecting existing regulatory frameworks while innovating within them—reflects the challenges of operating as a regulated institution in emerging markets.

**Authenticity and Connection**

Bessette emphasizes the "human nature of the interaction" and whether "we find a connection with the founder's vision and how they go about articulating it." She describes authenticity as "the smallest hill she'll always die on," indicating this criterion serves as a non-negotiable filter in partnership decisions.

**Ability to Challenge Traditional Thinking**

Successful partnerships require founders who can "help us think outside of the traditional lines of where we have traditionally operated." This criterion recognizes that innovation requires perspectives that challenge institutional assumptions while remaining grounded in practical implementation.

## Key Insights and Implications

1. **Tokenization Value Lies in Utility, Not Technology:** The technical process of creating tokens represents a minor component of successful tokenization. The majority of effort and value creation occurs in ecosystem development, relationship building, and creating genuine onchain utility that differentiates tokenized assets from traditional equivalents.

2. **Crisis as Catalyst for Structural Change:** Market crises accelerate adoption by revealing systemic vulnerabilities that tokenized solutions can address. The regional banking crisis and USDC depegging transformed tokenized money market funds from experimental products to essential infrastructure by highlighting the payments-yield gap in traditional systems.

3. **Phased Adoption Reflects Learning Curves:** The three-phase framework (Hold, Use, Build) acknowledges that both institutions and end-users require progressive exposure to onchain assets. Immediate full migration to native onchain systems would fail; instead, familiar wrappers (ETPs) serve as bridges while infrastructure and understanding develop.

4. **Ecosystem Development Exceeds Technical Implementation:** Fidelity's experience indicates that building the token represents less work than building the ecosystem for that token to function usefully. This has significant implications for timeline and resource allocation in tokenization projects.

5. **Regulatory Navigation Requires Dual Competency:** Successful operation in digital assets demands both deep understanding of existing regulatory frameworks and creative thinking about how those principles apply in novel contexts. This dual competency cannot be outsourced and must exist within partnerships.

6. **Institutional Adoption Drives Infrastructure Maturity:** Major asset managers entering the tokenization space create demand for robust infrastructure, regulatory clarity, and ecosystem development that benefits the entire market. Fidelity's participation signals and accelerates market maturation.

7. **Staking Yield as Bridge Product:** Packaging native onchain staking yield into traditional ETP wrappers exemplifies an effective strategy for introducing novel onchain functionality to traditional investors through familiar structures.

## Definitions and Terminology

**ETP (Exchange-Traded Product):** Investment vehicles that trade on exchanges and provide exposure to underlying assets. In this context, ETPs serve as traditional wrappers for onchain assets like Bitcoin, allowing conventional investors to gain exposure without directly holding cryptocurrency.

**Tokenization:** The process of representing real-world or traditional financial assets as digital tokens on a blockchain. Bessette emphasizes this must be accompanied by genuine onchain utility to create value.

**Tokenized Money Market Fund:** A money market fund represented as blockchain tokens, enabling onchain functionality while maintaining the underlying investment characteristics of traditional money market funds. These products bridge traditional finance and onchain ecosystems.

**RWA (Real-World Assets):** Traditional financial assets (such as treasuries, real estate, or commodities) that have been tokenized and brought onchain.

**Staking Yield:** Returns generated by participating in proof-of-stake blockchain networks through locking tokens to support network operations. Bessette describes this as "incredibly differentiated from traditional sources of yield."

**Onchain vs. Off-chain:** "Onchain" refers to activities, assets, or operations that occur directly on blockchain infrastructure. "Off-chain" refers to traditional systems and infrastructure. The progression from "on-chain assets in an off-chain wrapper" to fully onchain systems represents Fidelity's strategic evolution.

**USDC:** A stablecoin (cryptocurrency designed to maintain a stable value relative to the US dollar) that experienced a temporary depegging event during the regional banking crisis, catalyzing interest in tokenized money market funds as more stable reserve assets.

**Kaizen:** A Japanese business philosophy of continuous improvement that Fidelity applies to its digital asset strategy, emphasizing iterative learning and development.

**Genius Act:** Regulatory legislation (specific details not provided in the transcript) that enabled non-bank issuers to facilitate payments more easily, opening the marketplace for tokenized money market funds.
