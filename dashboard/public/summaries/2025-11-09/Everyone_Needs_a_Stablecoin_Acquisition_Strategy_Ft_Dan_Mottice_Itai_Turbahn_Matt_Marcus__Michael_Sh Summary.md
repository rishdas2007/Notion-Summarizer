## Executive Summary

This episode of the Tokenized podcast, published November 3, 2025, examines two major acquisitions in the stablecoin infrastructure space and their implications for the convergence of traditional fintech and crypto payment rails. The discussion centers on Modern Treasury's $40 million acquisition of Beam and Fireblocks' acquisition of Dynamic, both representing strategic moves to build comprehensive stablecoin payment infrastructure.

**Main Arguments:**

1. **Infrastructure Consolidation**: Major fintech and crypto infrastructure companies are acquiring specialized stablecoin capabilities to offer unified payment platforms that seamlessly integrate fiat and stablecoin rails.

2. **Market Maturation**: Stablecoin adoption is decoupling from cryptocurrency speculation, with volume growth driven by utility-focused use cases in remittances, payroll, and cross-border payments rather than trading activity.

3. **Interoperability Imperative**: The future of payments requires platforms that can orchestrate between traditional payment rails (ACH, RTP, FedNow) and stablecoin networks without requiring end-users to understand the underlying complexity.

4. **Enterprise Adoption Wave**: Traditional financial institutions, payment networks (Mastercard, Visa), and Web2 platforms are actively pursuing stablecoin strategies to address gaps in global payment coverage and reduce costs.

**Key Participants:**
- **Dan Mottice** (CEO & Co-Founder, Beam): Focused on instant on/off-ramp orchestration
- **Matt Marcus** (CEO & Co-Founder, Modern Treasury): Payment infrastructure and ledgering systems
- **Itai Turbahn** (Co-founder & CEO, Dynamic): Embedded wallet infrastructure
- **Michael Shaulov** (Co-Founder & CEO, Fireblocks): Digital asset custody and treasury management
- **Simon Taylor** (GTM, Tempo): Host and moderator

## Detailed Analysis

### Modern Treasury's Acquisition of Beam

Modern Treasury, a San Francisco-based payments infrastructure company that has enabled customers to move hundreds of billions of dollars, acquired Beam for a reported $40 million. This acquisition represents a strategic move to integrate stablecoin capabilities into Modern Treasury's existing fiat payment infrastructure.

**Modern Treasury's Core Business:**
Modern Treasury provides APIs that enable fintechs, marketplaces, and businesses to connect to the financial system. Their customer base includes companies like Procore (embedded payments), Built Rewards (rent payments), and Navon (T&E reimbursements). The company has established expertise in traditional payment rails including ACH, real-time payments (RTP), and FedNow.

**Beam's Specialized Capabilities:**
Beam focused on what Dan Mottice describes as "the orchestration layer" for instant on/off-ramps between stablecoins and instant payment rails. Specifically, Beam enabled:
- Instant conversion between stablecoins and fiat via Visa Direct
- Integration with RTP (Real-Time Payments)
- FedNow connectivity
- Work with customers like Sling Money for off-ramp flows

**Strategic Rationale:**
Matt Marcus articulated the acquisition logic: "We see a tremendous opportunity to provide modern infrastructure that works across fiat and stablecoins. And really, stablecoins is a way to improve on the legacy financial system, but it also needs to be interoperable with it."

The acquisition addresses several market needs:
1. Difficulty for fintechs in establishing banking partnerships due to changing regulatory landscape
2. Need for unified API covering both fiat and stablecoin payments
3. Demand for instant, low-cost cross-border payment capabilities
4. Requirement for robust ledgering infrastructure to prevent issues like those seen in the Synapse collapse

**Integration Plans:**
The immediate focus is achieving "one team, one dream, one code base" over the next 3-6 months, with plans to integrate Beam's capabilities into Modern Treasury's platform while maintaining service to existing Beam customers.

### Stablecoin Implementation Challenges and Solutions

Dan Mottice provided insight into the technical and operational challenges of implementing stablecoin infrastructure at scale:

**Key Challenges Identified:**
1. **Scalability**: Building systems capable of processing "hundreds of billions of dollars" in transaction volume
2. **Performance**: Ensuring instant settlement across multiple rails
3. **Simplification**: Making the technology accessible without requiring deep crypto expertise
4. **Regulatory Compliance**: Navigating licensing requirements across jurisdictions

**Solutions Approach:**
Beam's strategy involved learning from existing market enablers while developing proprietary approaches to simplify the technology stack. The goal is to make stablecoin integration as straightforward as traditional payment rail integration, abstracting away the complexity of blockchain interactions, gas payments, and wallet management.

### Convergence of Fintech and Crypto Rails

A central theme throughout the discussion was the inevitable convergence of traditional fintech infrastructure and crypto payment rails into unified financial infrastructure.

**Itai Turbahn's Perspective:**
"Fintech rails and crypto rails are pretty much like the same rails, right? They need to be intertwined. And over time, they're going to be more and more intertwined... The movement in and out of stablecoins needs to be accelerated. It shouldn't be a separate topic, right? Today, we talk about it as on-ramps and off-ramps. But in the future, it's just a thing that your fintech infrastructure provider should provide to you."

Turbahn expressed hope that future industry discourse would eliminate distinctions between "crypto conferences" and "fintech conferences," with all companies simply being fintech companies that may be "crypto first" in their technical approach.

**Practical Implications:**
The convergence means:
- Developers shouldn't need to explain on-ramps and off-ramps separately from core functionality
- Infrastructure providers should abstract away the complexity of multiple payment rails
- End-users should experience seamless money movement regardless of underlying technology
- The distinction between "crypto companies" and "fintech companies" should dissolve

### Stablecoin-Backed Accounts and Ledgering Infrastructure

Modern Treasury introduced the concept of "stablecoin-backed accounts" as an alternative to traditional FBO (For Benefit Of) accounts that fintechs use to hold customer funds.

**Product Concept:**
Matt Marcus explained: "That was the first foray we had into stablecoins was looking at stablecoin backed accounts, somewhat like an FBO account that a lot of these fintechs get. So a way to hold customer funds through a different vehicle."

**Key Benefits:**
1. **Accelerated Innovation**: Provides alternative to difficult-to-establish bank partnerships
2. **Interoperability**: Maintains ability to interact with fiat rails and traditional banking
3. **Orchestration**: Enables seamless movement between stablecoin and fiat
4. **Cross-Border Efficiency**: Treats currency conversion similar to FX operations

**Critical Infrastructure Component - Ledgering:**
Marcus emphasized the importance of robust ledgering systems, referencing the Synapse collapse where customers were locked out of accounts and some lost life savings due to alleged ledgering issues.

Modern Treasury's approach:
- Built the first ledger database product on the market
- Works with leading crypto exchanges and stablecoin issuers
- Treats ledgering as "a first class concern at scale"
- Provides visibility to fintechs for reconciliation and compliance

Marcus noted: "We see what it looks like before they use ledgers. And people aren't ledgering in a lot of cases. And so I think it's important as we look at like, how do you do stablecoin and fiat payments at scale, hold customer funds at scale, that we don't lose sight of that."

**Use Case Applications:**
The infrastructure supports various applications including:
- Stablecoin-linked cards
- Neobanks
- Payouts and payroll systems
- Corporate treasury management

### Mastercard's Potential Acquisition of ZeroHash

Fortune reported that Mastercard is in talks to acquire ZeroHash for approximately $1.5-2 billion, representing a significant move by a major payment network into stablecoin infrastructure.

**ZeroHash's Role:**
Itai Turbahn described ZeroHash's function: "Really, what ZeroHash does is moves money from fiat to stablecoins and from stablecoins to fiat. It does it at global scale and it does it in a compliant and very powerful way."

**Key Capabilities:**
- MTL (Money Transmitter License) licenses across multiple jurisdictions
- New York BitLicense
- Global licensing coverage with significant presence in Latin America
- Crypto brokerage-as-a-service capabilities
- Buy, sell, hold functionality for financial institutions

**Strategic Context:**
This potential acquisition follows reports of both Mastercard and Coinbase evaluating BVNK, another stablecoin infrastructure provider. The pattern suggests major payment networks are actively building stablecoin acquisition strategies.

**Mastercard's Historical Pattern:**
Dan Mottice noted: "Historically, MasterCard has been the first mover from a card network perspective as it relates to alternative ways of moving money. The Vocalink acquisition happened well before I think Visa entered alternative ways of moving money."

Vocalink, acquired by Mastercard, operates the UK's Faster Payments instant payment network, demonstrating Mastercard's willingness to invest in alternative payment infrastructure.

**Market Implications:**
Turbahn speculated on competitive dynamics: "I'm curious about what Visa does as a result of this, right? Because there aren't that many kind of players in the market left with that scale and that impact that a zero hash or a bridge or bvnk have."

The acquisition would position Mastercard to:
- Gain visibility into stablecoin money movement
- Serve financial institutions seeking crypto capabilities
- Build comprehensive fiat-to-stablecoin orchestration
- Leverage ZeroHash's extensive licensing infrastructure

### Stablecoin Volume Decoupling from Crypto Price Action

A significant market development discussed was the decoupling of stablecoin transaction volume from cryptocurrency price speculation, as documented in Andreessen Horowitz's 2025 crypto report.

**Key Data Points:**
- Visa reported stablecoin-linked card volumes up 4x quarter-over-quarter
- Stablecoin volumes represented approximately 2 basis points of Visa's 2024 volumes
- If growth continues, could reach 4, 8, 12+ basis points, becoming material to overall volume

**Shift in Narrative:**
Itai Turbahn emphasized: "Historically, we've talked about crypto as a financial asset. And now we've shifted the conversation to crypto at a technology rail. With things like L1s, like Tempo, etc., it's moving towards being essentially a very complementary, global-first, super-fast, super-cheap set of financial rails that you can leverage."

**Practical Evidence:**
Matt Marcus provided ground-level validation: "We see our customers who are fintechs moving money in the US, like escrow businesses, for example, coming to us every day asking about stablecoins. So it's very real. It's not tied to the asset prices. People are interested in the technology, what it means for their business."

**Money 2020 Conversations:**
Turbahn reported that discussions at the major fintech conference focused on utility questions:
- Can we remit money faster globally?
- Can we pay vendors more efficiently?
- Can we run a global-first neobank?

These represent fundamental business questions rather than speculative investment inquiries.

**Market Maturity Signal:**
Simon Taylor noted: "That was the signal I was looking for, which is your customers are asking you for it and they have nothing to do with crypto. They're not even necessarily the most tech forward in the world. They've just heard of the stablecoin thing and they want to know if it can help them operate their business differently."

### IBM's Partnership with DFNS

IBM announced a partnership with DFNS to create "Digital Asset Haven," a platform for managing crypto asset lifecycle within IBM Z-series mainframes.

**Technical Specifications:**
- Operates inside IBM Z-series mainframes (which process 70% of global financial transactions)
- Supports up to 40 public and private blockchains
- Includes programmable multi-party approvals
- Pre-integrated services for KYC, identity verification, AML, and yield generation

**Market Interpretation:**
Itai Turbahn, a former IBM Research employee, offered perspective: "My outside-in looking perspective, they probably have identified a lot of customers that are coming their way and saying, look, I also want to now move stablecoins. And for them, they have to choose a system that is an MPC, a multi-party computation type system to do that."

**Strategic Assessment:**
Turbahn characterized this as a market test rather than full commitment: "I read this much more in the lens of a giant corporation putting something out there at the first test to gauge market interest versus kind of an all-in IBM's now a stablecoin company."

**Success Metrics:**
The partnership should be evaluated based on follow-up announcements over coming months and years regarding deeper integration into IBM's core systems and expanded customer adoption.

### Fireblocks' Acquisition of Dynamic

Fireblocks acquired Dynamic to complete its infrastructure stack from institutional custody to consumer-facing embedded wallets.

**Dynamic's Core Offering:**
Itai Turbahn described Dynamic as "a wallet infrastructure company for developers." The platform enables developers to integrate non-custodial embedded wallets into applications within 10 minutes, supporting companies like Kraken, Stripe, and ZeroHash.

**Fireblocks' Strategic Vision:**
Michael Shaulov explained: "From our standpoint, now we basically have the full stack to enable [customers] to do anything from treasury, their backend wallets, their custody, and also then to essentially propagate all this functionality to their end users in a very, very simplified offering that from product and business standpoint gives them the fastest time to market."

**Complete Stack Components:**
1. **Treasury Operations**: Fireblocks UI for institutional management
2. **Backend Infrastructure**: Direct custody and key management services
3. **Developer Tools**: APIs for creating custodial and non-custodial experiences
4. **Consumer Layer**: Embedded wallets via Dynamic for end-user experiences

**Time-to-Market Advantage:**
Turbahn emphasized the speed benefit: "You can wake up in the morning as a developer, show up on the dynamic site and have a fully working set of wallets for your customers by midday... Today, you can wake up in the morning and by midday build a very basic version of a global cash app or a global Venmo app or a global remittance app."

**Abstraction Strategy:**
The roadmap focuses on progressive abstraction: "Can we take it from an hour to five minutes? Can we do it so that you don't have to set up five services in order to do on ramps in order to think about gas payments. The goal is that any Web2 developer... can they do it within five minutes without knowing anything about crypto?"

**Integration Philosophy:**
Turbahn articulated the goal: "We have lost the plot if we start talking to folks about gas payments or on-ramps or anything of that sort, right? And so the goal is to abstract more and more of that away, move that behind the veil, just in the sense that, you know, I don't show up and pitch you why ACH is better than the cart, right? It just kind of works in the background."

### Market Adoption Patterns and Future Outlook

Michael Shaulov provided insight into emerging adoption patterns based on Fireblocks' customer conversations.

**First Wave - International Money Movers:**
"The fastest now that is really, I think, massive wave that is now hitting... is basically all the international money movers, you know, companies like Ria and MoneyGram and all those guys, right? That's actually coming in as we speak."

**Second Wave - Web2 Platforms:**
Shaulov identified large tech platforms as the next major adopter segment, driven by specific pain points:

**Problem Statement:**
"The big tech platforms... have a very global contributors base, right, that they need to pay money to. And they want to understand how they're now leveraging stable coins to do it in real time at a low cost and with transparency."

**Geographic Coverage Gaps:**
While push-to-card and similar solutions work well for US and European corridors, Shaulov noted: "You want to push money to people in Indonesia and Philippines and in Africa or in Latin American countries, it's far from being solved and you would get like different SLAs and different cost structures and that variable user experience or variable SLA is part of the reasons why it's pushing them to look into stable coins as a first order solution."

**Two-Order Value Proposition:**
1. **First Order**: Access to previously underserved markets with consistent SLAs
2. **Second Order**: Programmability and automation capabilities

**Dan Mottice's Vision:**
Looking forward 3-6 months and beyond, Mottice outlined the next frontier: "We've yet to see what it looks like when an orchestration platform seamlessly bridges together multiple jurisdictions... nobody's really bridged together all of the above. And in addition, reached into Asia and really assembled this kind of seamless mesh settlement network."

**Derivative Capabilities:**
Once global orchestration is achieved:
- On-chain FX becomes viable
- Majority of global commerce can exist on-chain
- New use cases emerge that haven't been conceived yet

**Market Stage Assessment:**
Mottice concluded: "We're really still in kind of like inning one of many here as it relates to global adoption and true institutional adoption and migration of the vast majority of capital flows actually happens in an on-chain environment."

## Key Insights and Implications

### 1. Acquisition Strategy as Competitive Necessity

The episode title "Everyone Needs a Stablecoin Acquisition Strategy" reflects a fundamental market shift. Major players across fintech, payments, and financial services are recognizing that building stablecoin capabilities organically may be too slow. Strategic acquisitions enable:
- Immediate access to specialized expertise and technology
- Accelerated time-to-market for integrated offerings
- Acquisition of regulatory licenses and compliance infrastructure
- Access to existing customer relationships and use cases

### 2. The Abstraction Imperative

A recurring theme was the need to abstract away blockchain complexity. Success in mainstream adoption requires that end-users and even developers don't need to understand:
- Gas payments and blockchain mechanics
- Wallet types and custody models
- On-ramp and off-ramp processes
- Differences between chains and protocols

This abstraction must occur at the infrastructure layer, not the application layer, suggesting significant value creation opportunity for middleware providers.

### 3. Ledgering as Critical Infrastructure

The emphasis on ledgering systems, particularly in light of the Synapse collapse, highlights an often-overlooked component of payment infrastructure. As stablecoin volumes scale, the ability to accurately track, reconcile, and audit transactions becomes paramount. Companies lacking robust ledgering capabilities face existential risk.

### 4. Geographic Arbitrage Opportunity

The discussion revealed that stablecoins' primary value proposition for many enterprises is not speed or cost in developed markets (where solutions exist) but rather:
- Consistent global coverage regardless of corridor
- Uniform SLAs across all markets
- Predictable cost structures
- Access to underbanked regions (Indonesia, Philippines, Africa, Latin America)

This suggests the highest-value use cases may be in emerging markets rather than developed economies.

### 5. Regulatory Licensing as Moat

Multiple participants emphasized the importance of regulatory licenses (MTLs, BitLicense, international permissions) as competitive advantages. ZeroHash's extensive licensing coverage was cited as a key strategic asset. This suggests that regulatory compliance infrastructure may be as valuable as technical infrastructure in determining market winners.

### 6. The "Rail Above Rails" Concept

Simon Taylor's framing of stablecoins as a "rail above rails" captures the architectural vision: a global settlement layer that can interoperate with all existing payment rails (ACH, RTP, FedNow, SWIFT, card networks) while also enabling direct peer-to-peer transactions. This positions stablecoins not as replacement infrastructure but as orchestration layer.

### 7. Decoupling Signals Market Maturity

The decoupling of stablecoin volume from crypto price action represents a fundamental market maturation. When utility-driven demand exceeds speculation-driven demand, it indicates:
- Real business problems being solved
- Sustainable revenue models emerging
- Reduced correlation with crypto market cycles
- Increased institutional confidence

### 8. Speed of Integration as Competitive Advantage

The emphasis on "10 minutes to integration" or "midday deployment" reflects that developer experience and time-to-market are becoming primary competitive differentiators. In a rapidly evolving market, the ability to experiment quickly and iterate based on customer feedback creates significant advantage.

## Definitions and Terminology

**Stablecoin**: A cryptocurrency designed to maintain a stable value relative to a reference asset, typically the US dollar. Stablecoins like USDC and USDT are backed by reserves of fiat currency.

**On-ramp/Off-ramp**: The process of converting between fiat currency and cryptocurrency (on-ramp) or cryptocurrency back to fiat (off-ramp). The industry is moving toward abstracting these terms away from user-facing language.

**Orchestration Layer**: Infrastructure that coordinates and manages transactions across multiple payment rails and blockchain networks, handling routing, conversion, and settlement logic.

**FBO Account (For Benefit Of)**: A bank account held by one party for the benefit of another, commonly used by fintechs to hold customer funds.

**Ledgering/Ledger Database**: A system for recording and tracking all financial transactions with immutability and auditability. Critical for reconciliation and compliance.

**RTP (Real-Time Payments)**: The US instant payment network operated by The Clearing House, enabling immediate fund transfers 24/7/365.

**FedNow**: The Federal Reserve's instant payment service launched in 2023, providing real-time payment and settlement infrastructure.

**Visa Direct**: Visa's real-time push payment platform enabling near-instant fund transfers to eligible cards and accounts.

**MPC (Multi-Party Computation)**: A cryptographic technique allowing multiple parties to jointly compute a function while keeping inputs private, used in wallet security and key management.

**Embedded Wallet**: A cryptocurrency wallet integrated directly into an application's user interface, abstracting away the complexity of separate wallet management.

**Non-custodial Wallet**: A wallet where the user maintains control of private keys, as opposed to custodial wallets where a third party holds the keys.

**MTL (Money Transmitter License)**: State-level licenses required in the US to operate as a money transmitter, with varying requirements across jurisdictions.

**BitLicense**: New York State's regulatory framework for cryptocurrency businesses, known for stringent requirements.

**ACH (Automated Clearing House)**: The US electronic network for financial transactions, typically processing in batches with 1-2 day settlement.

**Push-to-Card**: Payment method that sends funds directly to a debit card, enabling faster access than traditional ACH.

**L1 (Layer 1)**: Base blockchain protocols like Ethereum, Solana, or Tempo that provide the foundational infrastructure for applications.

**Gas Payments**: Transaction fees paid on blockchain networks to compensate validators for processing transactions.

**Corridor**: In payments, a specific route between two countries or regions (e.g., US-to-Mexico corridor).

**SLA (Service Level Agreement)**: Contractual commitment regarding service performance, including metrics like transaction speed and success rates.
